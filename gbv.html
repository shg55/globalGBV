What is GBV?

ABOUT

The issue of gender-based violence (GBV) has been around for many decades and is deeply rooted in our societies. It is one of the most frequent violations against women as the World Bank estimates that at least 35% of women worldwide have experienced physical or sexual violence.

A FEW TYPES OF GBV


Physical Violence

Known as the intentional use of physical force against another person or group of people, it can result in injuries, psychological harm, maldevelopment, or even death. Of all the women killed by homicide, half were killed by intimate partners of family members in 2012.

Sexual Violence

It includes any sexual act or any attempt of sexual acts against a person using coercion by any person in any setting. It includes any sexual act or any attempt of sexual acts against a person using coercion by any person in any setting.

Economic Violence

It can cause economic harm to a person, such as the loss of property. 23% of female undergraduate university students reported assault that affected their work life in 2015.

Cyber Violence

Through online resources like social networks, it can cause psychological harm to a person. 1 in 10 women in the European Union have reported cyber-harassment since the age of 15. Cyber-harassment includes unwanted, offensive sexually explicit emails/SMS or offensive, inappropriate advances on social media.

Activism Efforts

The 21st Century is making everyone more aware of gender-based violence (GBV). Protests have erupted throughout the world, demanding justice for victims of GBV. For example, the #TotalShutDown protest in South Africa urges every human to realize that "her body is not your crime scene." Furthermore, female empowerment is also being encouraged in the battle against GBV. In Rwanda and in Delhi, gender desks have been created and run by women 24 hours every day in order to help victims of GBV within police stations. These measures have fostered security and safety, according to Mary Kimani, a journalist for Online AfricaRenewal. Laws are being reformed as well. The United Nations Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women persuades governments to change discriminatory practices and laws like early marriage. Furthermore, the Africa-UNiTE campaign aims to end GBV in Africa and pushes governments to make legislation against GBV. Schools, universities, and private companies have started to educate their people about the repercussions of gender discrimination and sexual harassment in Africa. A significant organization that famous for fighting to stop violence against women is known as CARE. It has worked in 93 countries, reaching 63 million people through 950 poverty-fighting development and humanitarian aid programs, according to its website.

How you can help!


Lifelines you can contact if you are in the United States


  • Rape, Abuse & Incest National Network (RAINN): RAINN is the United States' largest anti-sexual violence lifeline. Click here to access their website for more information. Call 800.656.4673 for help.
  • Love is Respect: a project of the National Domestic Violence Hotline, its motto is to empower teenagers to prevent domestic violence within relationships. Click here to access their website for more information. Call 1.866.331.9474 for help.
  • Suicide Prevention Lifeline: call 800-273-8255 if you know or are in danger of committing suicide. Click here to access its website. Spanish speakers should reach out to 888-628-9454 while those who have difficulty hearing can call 800-799-4889.
  • Crisis Text Line: if you are unable to reach a website or physically call a number, you can text HOME to 741741 in order to talk to a trained counselor.

If you are a victim and survivor of GBV


  • Know that you are NOT alone
  • Tell your stories to someone. Whether it be to your loved ones or an online lifeline support, it is important that you do not go through this alone.
  • If you live in the United States, vote in all elections for candidates who want to help survivors of abuse and GBV.
  • If you have children, teach them what consent means and what it means to be respectful of boundaries.
  • Attend rallies in support of helping victims of GBV. The only way change can happen in your life is if you take action.

If you are not a victim of GBV


  • Listen to the survivors. Regardless of how long it takes for them to tell their story to you, you have to listen to them. The worst thing you can do to a survivor is to say that their stories and feelings are invalid and unbelievable.
  • Donate to one of the previously mentioned lifelines. It is important for them to get money to keep their programs running if we want to reduce GBV in our world.
  • If you live in the United States, vote in all elections for candidates who want to help survivors of abuse and GBV. You have a voice in bringing a change in legislation.
  • If you have children, teach them what consent means and what it means to be respectful of boundaries. This is crucial if we want to alleviate the issue of GBV.
  • Attend rallies in support of helping victims of GBV. The only way change can happen in your life is if you take action.

GBV in Africa

FAST FACTS

  • 45.6% of African women 15 years or older experienced physical or sexual violence or both.
  • 56% of Tanzanian women and 71% of Ethiopian women have faced some form of violence by their intimate partners.
  • A South African woman is killed by an intimate partner every 6 hours.
  • The sexual offense rate of women in South Africa increased by 53% in 2017.
  • 3 women die at the hands of their partners every day in South Africa due to gender-based violence.
  • In South Africa, femicide in 2015 was 9.6%, four times higher than the global rate of 2.4 per 100,000 women.
  • In 2017, an average of 109.1 rapes was recorded every day in South Africa.

Many factors cause gender-based violence in Africa, such as a lack of education and child maltreatment. Women are not equipped to handle these dire situations, in turn hurting their mental, physical, sexual, and reproductive health. In fact, women who face gender-based violence are twice as likely to be depressed, abuse alcohol, have human Immunodeficiency virus (HIV), and other sexually transmitted diseases.

Unfortunately, in order to prove that a man has committed gender-based violence in Africa, a victim must show hard-evidence of the violence, or else police officers will not shed a glance at the victim. The fear of facing a police officer has led many women to keep their stories a secret. Furthermore, a high possibility of being raped by police officers, who are meant to protect their people, is not uncommon in poor African countries, according to Mary Wandia, a Kenyan gender advocate.

GBV in Asia

FAST FACTS

  • A 2010 study in New Delhi showed that 66% of women have reported sexual harassment between two and five times in 2009.
  • Over the past decade, 2.24 million crimes against women were reported in India alone. This means that 26 crimes are reported every hour or one per two minutes.
  • In a study of 143 gender-based violence survivors, 56% of Filipinas and 64% Indian and Pakistani women said that they experienced some form of sexual violence.
  • In 2014 and 2015, close to 54% of all abductions of women were carried out to force them into marriage and unwanted pregnancies.

It is important to realize that it is difficult for women to speak up against crime, as they will be subjected to social isolation and scorn. Similar to African police officers, many Asian police officers choose not to register a woman's complaint. In Asia, the idea of familial ties is stressed: this means that there are more reports of domestic, family violence. In fact, the top explanations behind domestic violence are that the husband wants to assert his dominance so that he can have more power and control. Additionally, Asian cultures heavily portray women to be calm, patient, and feminine. What this means, unfortunately, is that women are assumed to be an easy target for men to take their anger out on.

GBV in Australia

FAST FACTS

  • 87% of gender-based violence victims have anonymously reported that the perpetrator was someone they knew, whether it be an intimate partner, family member, or friend.
  • 2 in 5 people aged 18 and over have experienced gender-based violence since the age of 15.
  • 1 in 2 women has experienced sexual harassment during her lifetime.
  • Women are not the only ones who face violence. In fact, 1 in 4 men has experienced sexual harassment during their lifetime in Australia.

GBV in Europe

FAST FACTS

  • Since the age of 15, 55% of women have experienced sexual harassment in some form, like unwelcome touching, hugging, or kissing.
  • 20% of women have experienced physical violence by someone other than their intimate partner.
  • Around 75% of women in qualified professions have experienced sexual harassment.
  • 1 in 3 women has experienced physical and sexual violence while 1 in 2 women has experienced sexual harassment.

Even though gender-based violence is less prominent in Europe, only 33% of victims have chosen to contact the police or some other organization after some kind of gender-based violence. The impacts of gender-based violence continue to haunt victims: 21% of victims continue to suffer from panic attacks while 35% become depressed. Furthermore, 43% of women have had difficulty in following relationships after an act of intimate domestic violence.

GBV in Latin America

FAST FACTS

  • 41% of women have experienced physical abuse and 10% have experienced sexual violence at the hands of their partners, according to a 2004-2005 Peru study.
  • 18% of girls in the Caribbean have been sexually abused before the age of 16.
  • More than 50% of women in Nicaragua are victims of intimate partner violence.
  • In 12 percent of the reported cases of gender-based violence in El Salvador, the perpetrators were men of the law, like lawyers and police officers.

Latin America is home to the underlying issues of gender-based violence. 14 out of 25 countries in Latin America have the highest rates of femicide in the world. Laws and practices to convict perpetrators of gender-based violence are very weak as the patriarchal system continues to exist in high poverty and crime areas. Moreover, the drug wars have made femicide and gender-based violence rates much higher, as dangerous gangs rape women who do not give them the drugs they want. This, unfortunately, is used as a tactic to scare other gangs or initiate men into their gang.

GBV in North America

FAST FACTS

  • Each year, 10 million men and women in the United States face physical abuse from their partners.
  • More than 7 women are victims of gender-related killings each day.
  • 1 in 3 women and 1 in 4 men are victims of physical abuse from their partners.
  • 1 in 6 American women are victims of rape.
  • 40% of Canadian women experience sexual assault as early as the age of 16.
  • 1 in 71 men has been raped in his life.

In places like North America, where there are more economic opportunities and rights for women, it is possible for men to be victims of violence as well. While the opportunities to report an incident of gender-based violence are increasing, only 12% of college survivors have reported, and within this 12%, 1 in 4 has faced a threat of arrest.
